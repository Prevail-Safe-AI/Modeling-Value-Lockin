Natural selection is the process by which populations of living organisms adapt and change over time in response to their environment. It's a key mechanism of evolution.

Here's how it works:

1. Variation: All living organisms have genetic variations, which are differences in their DNA that can affect their traits.
2. Heritability: These traits are heritable, meaning they're passed down from parents to offspring.
3. Environment: The environment puts pressure on the population, making it harder for some individuals to survive and reproduce.
4. Differential reproduction: The individuals with traits that are better suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those traits on to their offspring.
5. Accumulation: Over many generations, these small advantages can accumulate, leading to the evolution of new traits and adaptations.

The mechanisms involved in natural selection include:

* Genetic drift: Random changes in the frequency of traits in a population.
* Gene flow: The movement of genes from one population to another.
* Mutation: Changes in the DNA sequence of an individual.
* Genetic recombination: The shuffling of genes during reproduction.

These mechanisms can lead to the evolution of new species, the adaptation of populations to their environment, and the loss of traits that are no longer beneficial.
