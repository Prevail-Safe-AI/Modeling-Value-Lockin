Genetic variation and mutation are the raw materials for natural selection to act upon. Here's how it works:

2. Mutation: Mutations are changes in the DNA sequence that can occur spontaneously or as a result of environmental factors like radiation or chemicals. These changes can be neutral, beneficial, or detrimental to the organism.
3. Natural selection: When an organism's environment changes, the existing genetic variation and mutation rates affect the organism's ability to survive and reproduce. Organisms with beneficial traits (e.g., adaptations to the new environment) are more likely to survive and pass those traits to their offspring. Conversely, organisms with detrimental traits are more likely to die off or fail to reproduce.
4. Adaptation: Over time, the accumulation of beneficial mutations and the selection of those traits leads to the adaptation of the population to its environment. This is because the population is becoming more genetically diverse, with a greater proportion of individuals possessing traits that are better suited to the environment.

A mutation might occur that gives some birds a slightly darker plumage, which helps them blend in with the darker foliage. If this trait is beneficial, natural selection will favor those birds, and they'll be more likely to survive and reproduce, passing the trait to their offspring. Over generations, the population will become more adapted to the changing environment, with a greater proportion of birds having darker plumage.

In summary, genetic variation and mutation provide the raw materials for natural selection to act upon, and the interaction between these processes enables living organisms to adapt to their environments over time.
