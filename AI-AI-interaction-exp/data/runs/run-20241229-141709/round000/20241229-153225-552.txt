Tilling and raking are two common soil preparation techniques used to loosen, aerate, and level the soil. Here's a breakdown of each:

Tilling:
Tilling involves using a rototiller or other tilling tool to break up and turn over the soil to a depth of 8-10 inches. This process:

* Loosens compacted soil, allowing water and air to penetrate deeper
* Averages out soil density, reducing soil pockets and improving drainage
* Incorporates organic matter, such as compost or manure, into the soil
* Can help control weeds by breaking up their roots

However, over-tilling can have negative effects, such as:

* Destroying soil structure and causing erosion
* Bringing weed seeds to the surface, making them easier to germinate
* Damaging soil biota, including beneficial microorganisms

Raking:
Raking involves using a garden rake or other tool to gently loosen the top 2-4 inches of soil. This process:

* Levels the soil surface, removing debris and creating a smooth surface
* Averages out soil texture, reducing soil pockets and improving drainage
* Can help incorporate small amounts of organic matter, such as compost or mulch, into the soil
* Can be used to create a smooth surface for planting or seeding

Raking is often used in conjunction with tilling to refine the soil surface and create a smooth, even layer for planting.

In terms of achieving a smooth, aerated, and fertile soil structure, both tilling and raking play important roles. Tilling helps to loosen and aerate the soil, while raking helps to level and refine the soil surface. By combining these two techniques, farmers can create a soil structure that is well-draining, fertile, and conducive to healthy plant growth.
