When we focus our attention on something, it's not just one area of the brain that's active, but rather a network of regions that work together to process and prioritize information.

The key brain regions involved in attention include:

* The prefrontal cortex (PFC), which is responsible for executive functions such as decision-making, planning, and problem-solving.
* The parietal lobe, which is involved in spatial attention and processing visual information.
* The temporal lobe, which is involved in auditory attention and processing auditory information.
* The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), which is involved in conflict monitoring and error detection.
* The basal ganglia, which is involved in movement planning and execution.

When we focus our attention on something, these regions work together to:

* Filter out irrelevant information (inhibitory processes)
* Enhance relevant information (excitatory processes)
* Coordinate movement and action (motor planning)
* Monitor for errors and conflicts (conflict monitoring)

The process of attention is often described as a "spotlight" that moves across the visual field, highlighting relevant information and ignoring irrelevant information. This spotlight is thought to be controlled by the prefrontal cortex, which sends signals to other brain regions to focus attention on specific stimuli.

In terms of neural networks, attention is thought to involve the coordination of multiple networks, including:

* The default mode network (DMN), which is active when we're not focused on the outside world and is involved in mind-wandering and daydreaming.
* The salience network (SN), which is involved in detecting and responding to salient stimuli.
* The central executive network (CEN), which is involved in executive functions such as planning, decision-making, and problem-solving.
