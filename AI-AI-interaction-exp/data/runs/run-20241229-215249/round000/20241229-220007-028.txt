Evaluating good faith in an agreement or contract can be a nuanced task. Here are some factors to consider:

1. Prior interactions: Yes, examine the parties' past dealings, including any previous agreements, communications, and behaviors. This can help you understand their intentions and whether they've demonstrated a pattern of honesty and fairness.
2. Context: Consider the circumstances surrounding the agreement's creation, including any external pressures, biases, or influences that might have impacted the parties' decisions.
3. Communication: Review the communication process leading up to the agreement. Were the parties transparent, clear, and respectful in their interactions? Were there any red flags or concerns that were ignored or dismissed?
4. Power imbalance: Identify any potential power imbalances between the parties, such as significant differences in size, resources, or expertise. This can affect the negotiation process and the agreement's terms.
5. Unusual or one-sided terms: Be cautious of agreements with terms that seem overly favorable to one party or contain unusual clauses. This could indicate a lack of good faith or an attempt to take advantage of the other party.
6. Parties' motivations: Consider the motivations behind the agreement. Are the parties acting in their own best interests, or are there other factors at play, such as a desire to avoid conflict or maintain a relationship?
7. Compliance with laws and regulations: Ensure the agreement complies with relevant laws, regulations, and industry standards. Non-compliance could indicate a lack of good faith.
8. Parties' reputation: Research the parties' reputation and track record in similar agreements or transactions. A party with a history of dishonest or unfair behavior may be more likely to act in bad faith.
9. Negotiation process: Evaluate the negotiation process itself. Were the parties given equal opportunities to negotiate, or was one party dominant? Were there any attempts to intimidate, manipulate, or deceive the other party?
10. Post-agreement behavior: Monitor the parties' behavior after the agreement is signed. Do they act in accordance with the agreement's terms, or do they try to renege or exploit loopholes?

When assessing these factors, consider the specific circumstances and context of the agreement. It's not a one-size-fits-all approach, and you may need to weigh the importance of each factor depending on the situation.

Keep in mind that evaluating good faith is often subjective and may require a deep understanding of the parties involved, the agreement's terms, and the context in which it was created.
