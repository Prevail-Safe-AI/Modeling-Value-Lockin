Natural selection is a fundamental concept in evolutionary biology. It's a process that drives the evolution of populations over time by favoring the survival and reproduction of individuals with traits that are better suited to their environment.

The key mechanisms involved in natural selection are:

1. Variation: There must be genetic variation within a population for natural selection to act upon. This can arise from mutations, genetic recombination, or gene flow.
2. Heritability: The traits being selected for must be heritable, meaning they are passed down from parents to offspring through the transmission of genes.
3. Environment: The environment must exert a selective pressure on the population, favoring individuals with certain traits over others.
4. Selection: The environment selects for certain traits, leading to a difference in survival and reproduction rates between individuals with those traits and those without.
5. Differential reproduction: As a result of selection, individuals with the favored traits tend to reproduce more than those without, leading to an increase in the frequency of those traits in the population.
6. Evolution: Over time, this process of selection and differential reproduction leads to the evolution of the population, as the frequency of the favored traits increases and the population adapts to its environment.

There are many examples of natural selection in action. One classic example is the peppered moth in England. Prior to the Industrial Revolution, the moths had a light-colored, speckled appearance that allowed them to blend in with the lichen-covered trees they rested on. These dark-colored moths were better camouflaged on the dark trees and had a selective advantage over the light-colored moths. As a result, the frequency of the dark-colored trait increased in the population over time.

When antibiotics are introduced into a population of bacteria, those that are resistant to the antibiotic have a selective advantage over those that are not. As a result, the frequency of the resistant trait increases in the population over time, leading to the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.
