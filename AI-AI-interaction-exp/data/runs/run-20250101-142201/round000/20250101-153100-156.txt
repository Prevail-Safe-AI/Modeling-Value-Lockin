"In-situ" is a Latin phrase that means "in the original place" or "on site". In the context of data collection, "in-situ" refers to the direct measurement or observation of a phenomenon, process, or sample in its natural or original location, without moving or relocating it.

For example, collecting water quality data by measuring the pH level of a river directly in the river, rather than collecting a sample and analyzing it in a laboratory, would be an in-situ data collection method.

In-situ data collection is often preferred when studying dynamic or ephemeral phenomena, or when the sample or environment is sensitive to handling or transportation. It can also provide more accurate and representative data, as the measurement is made in the exact context in which the phenomenon occurs.

So, to answer your question, "in-situ" is not a specific methodology or type of measurement, but rather a way of collecting data directly in the original location, without altering or manipulating the environment or sample.
