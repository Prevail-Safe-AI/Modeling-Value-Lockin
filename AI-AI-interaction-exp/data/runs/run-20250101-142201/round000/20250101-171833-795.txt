The global workspace theory (GWT) is a cognitive architecture proposed by psychologist Bernard Baars. According to GWT, consciousness arises from the global workspace, which is a network of highly interconnected regions in the brain.

The global workspace is thought to be responsible for integrating information from various sensory, motor, and cognitive systems. It's like a "workspace" where information from different parts of the brain is brought together, processed, and made available to other parts of the brain.

In this context, "global" refers to the fact that the workspace is a distributed network that spans multiple brain regions, rather than a single localized area. The workspace is thought to be responsible for:

1. Global broadcasting: Information from various sources is broadcasted to the global workspace, making it available to other parts of the brain.
2. Global access: The global workspace provides access to information from all parts of the brain, allowing different regions to communicate and interact with each other.
3. Global control: The global workspace is thought to be responsible for controlling the flow of information between different brain regions, allowing for the integration of information and the generation of conscious experience.

GWT suggests that consciousness arises when information from various brain regions is integrated in the global workspace. This integration is thought to give rise to the unity, coherence, and subjective experience of consciousness.

In terms of how GWT relates to the integration of diverse brain regions' information, it's thought that the global workspace acts as a kind of "hub" that integrates information from multiple sources. This integration is thought to be necessary for the generation of conscious experience, as it allows for the combination of information from different senses, memories, and cognitive processes.

GWT is often contrasted with other theories of consciousness, such as Integrated Information Theory (IIT), which suggests that consciousness arises from the integrated information generated by the causal interactions within the brain. While GWT and IIT are distinct theories, they share some similarities, and some researchers have suggested that they may be complementary rather than mutually exclusive.
