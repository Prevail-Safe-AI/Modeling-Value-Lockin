"Made in good faith" refers to an agreement or contract where both parties intend to fulfill their obligations and are not attempting to deceive or harm each other. It implies a genuine and sincere intention to uphold the terms of the agreement.

In practice, a good faith agreement or contract typically involves:

1. Honesty: Both parties are truthful and transparent in their communication and negotiations.
2. Fairness: The agreement is reasonable and equitable, with no hidden agendas or unfair advantages.
3. Mutual understanding: Both parties have a clear understanding of the terms and expectations.
4. No coercion: The agreement is not the result of duress, intimidation, or undue influence.

To determine if an agreement or contract was made in good faith, you can consider the following criteria:

1. The parties' intentions: Were they genuinely trying to reach a mutually beneficial agreement?
2. The negotiation process: Was the negotiation fair, open, and transparent?
3. The terms of the agreement: Are the terms reasonable, clear, and free from ambiguity?
4. The parties' behavior: Have they consistently acted in good faith since the agreement was made?

If someone fails to honor an agreement or contract made in good faith, the consequences can vary depending on the specific circumstances. However, common consequences might include:

1. Legal action: The injured party may seek legal remedies, such as damages or specific performance.
2. Reputation damage: The party that failed to honor the agreement may suffer reputational damage and loss of trust.
3. Loss of future opportunities: The party may miss out on future business or partnership opportunities due to their breach of trust.

Keep in mind that the specific consequences will depend on the context and the laws governing the agreement or contract.
