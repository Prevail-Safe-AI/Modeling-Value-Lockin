Natural selection is a fundamental concept in biology, and understanding it can be challenging. Here's a simplified explanation:

Natural selection is the process by which the environment favors the survival and reproduction of individuals with certain traits that are better suited to their environment. This leads to the accumulation of these traits over generations, resulting in the evolution of the species.

Here's a step-by-step breakdown:

1. Variation: All living organisms exhibit genetic variation, which means they have different traits, such as height, eye color, or beak shape.
2. Environment: The environment presents challenges and opportunities that affect an organism's ability to survive and reproduce. For example, a harsh climate, predators, or competition for resources.
3. Selection: The environment "selects" for certain traits that are beneficial for survival and reproduction. This means that individuals with these traits are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing their traits to their offspring.
4. Heritability: The traits that are beneficial for survival are heritable, meaning they are determined by the organism's genes. This means that the offspring of individuals with beneficial traits are more likely to inherit those traits.
5. Adaptation: Over time, the accumulation of beneficial traits leads to the adaptation of the species to its environment. This can result in the evolution of new species or the modification of existing ones.

As for traits that are not beneficial, they are less likely to be passed on to the next generation. This is because individuals with these traits may not survive or reproduce as successfully, reducing the likelihood of their genes being inherited.

To illustrate this, consider the example of the peppered moth. Before the Industrial Revolution, the moths had a light-colored, speckled appearance that allowed them to blend in with the lichen-covered trees they rested on. However, as the trees became darker due to pollution, a genetic variation in the moth population resulted in some individuals having a dark-colored appearance. These dark-colored moths were better camouflaged on the dark trees and were more likely to survive and reproduce, passing their genes to their offspring. Over time, the population shifted towards the dark-colored trait, illustrating the process of natural selection.
