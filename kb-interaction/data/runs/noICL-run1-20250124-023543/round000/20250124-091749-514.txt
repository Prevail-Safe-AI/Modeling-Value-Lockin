Global Workspace Theory (GWT) is a cognitive architecture that explains how our brain processes information. It was developed by psychologist Bernard Baars in the 1980s. The theory proposes that our brain has a "workspace" or a "global workspace" that integrates information from various sensory and memory systems.

Think of the global workspace as a central hub or a "broadband network" that connects different parts of the brain. When we perceive or think about something, the relevant information is sent to this workspace, where it's processed and integrated with other information.

Now, about the neural oscillations part: Neural oscillations refer to the synchronized electrical activity of neurons in the brain. Different frequencies of oscillations are associated with different cognitive processes, such as attention, memory, and decision-making.

In the context of GWT, synchronized neural oscillations play a crucial role in integrating information from different parts of the brain. When we're processing information, different brain regions communicate with each other through neural oscillations. For example, when we're paying attention to a specific sound, the auditory cortex and the prefrontal cortex (responsible for attention) start oscillating at a specific frequency, allowing them to communicate and process the information together.

Here's an example to illustrate how GWT helps our brains process information:

Imagine you're trying to solve a puzzle. You're looking at a piece of paper with a complex pattern, and you're trying to figure out how the pieces fit together. Your brain is processing visual information from the pattern, and you're also using your working memory to hold onto the pieces and their relationships.

According to GWT, the global workspace is the "hub" where all this information is integrated. The visual cortex sends the pattern information to the global workspace, and the working memory system sends the pieces and their relationships. The global workspace then integrates this information, allowing you to make connections between the pieces and solve the puzzle.

The synchronized neural oscillations help facilitate this integration by allowing different brain regions to communicate and process the information together. It's like a symphony of neural activity, where different instruments (brain regions) play together in harmony to create a beautiful melody (the integrated information).
