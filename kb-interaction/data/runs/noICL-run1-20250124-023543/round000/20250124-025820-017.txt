Utilitarianism is a moral philosophy that aims to maximize overall happiness or well-being. The core idea is that an action is right if it leads to the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people. This can be applied to individual decisions, social policies, or even global issues.

Alternatively, they could allocate the funds to improve the water infrastructure in a different part of the city, which would benefit a larger number of people but not address the immediate crisis.

From a utilitarian perspective, the decision to build the new water treatment plant would be considered the "right" choice, as it would provide a greater overall benefit to the majority of the city's residents. However, this approach neglects the individual rights of the displaced residents, who would be forced to leave their homes and communities.

This is where the limitations of utilitarianism come into play. Critics argue that prioritizing overall happiness can lead to the neglect of individual rights, dignity, and autonomy. In this scenario, the displaced residents' rights to their homes, livelihoods, and cultural heritage are being sacrificed for the greater good.

In practical terms, utilitarianism can play out in various ways, such as:

1. Sacrificing individual freedoms for the sake of collective benefits, like mandatory vaccination policies or restrictions on personal freedoms during times of crisis.
2. Prioritizing economic growth over environmental concerns, leading to environmental degradation and harm to specific communities.
3. Implementing policies that benefit the majority, but disproportionately harm marginalized groups, such as racial or socioeconomic minorities.

To mitigate these limitations, some philosophers have proposed modifications to utilitarianism, such as:

1. Incorporating individual rights and dignity into the decision-making process.
2. Considering the distribution of benefits and burdens, ensuring that the greatest happiness is achieved in a fair and equitable manner.
3. Focusing on long-term consequences and considering the potential harm caused by short-term gains.
