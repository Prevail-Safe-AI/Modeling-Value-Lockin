Natural selection is the process by which individuals with certain traits that are better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those traits on to their offspring. This leads to the evolution of populations over time.

Here's a more detailed explanation:

1. Variation: All living organisms exhibit genetic variation, which means they have different traits, such as height, color, or shape.
2. Heritability: These traits are heritable, meaning they are passed down from parents to offspring through genes.
3. Environment: The environment presents challenges and opportunities that affect an organism's ability to survive and reproduce.
4. Selection: The environment selects for certain traits that are better suited to it. For example, in a hot desert, organisms with darker skin may be more likely to survive because they can absorb more heat.
5. Differential reproduction: As a result of the selection, individuals with the favored traits are more likely to reproduce, passing those traits on to their offspring.
6. Evolution: Over time, this process leads to the evolution of the population, as the favored traits become more common.

Examples of natural selection can be seen in many species and environments. Here are a few:

* Peppered moths: In England, the peppered moth population evolved from light-colored to dark-colored over the course of a few decades due to the industrial revolution's pollution, which darkened tree trunks. The dark-colored moths were better camouflaged and had a selective advantage.
* Antibiotic resistance: Bacteria that are resistant to antibiotics are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on their resistance genes to their offspring. This has led to the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.
* Finches: On the Galapagos Islands, finches with larger beaks were better able to crack open tough seeds during a drought, giving them a selective advantage.

These are just a few examples of how natural selection has been observed and proven in different species and environments.
