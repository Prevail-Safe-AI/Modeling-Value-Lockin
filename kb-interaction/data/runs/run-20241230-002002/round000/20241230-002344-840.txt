Let's break it down.

Tissues are formed from cells through a process called differentiation. Differentiation is the process by which a cell becomes specialized to perform a specific function. This occurs when a cell receives specific signals, such as hormones or growth factors, that trigger changes in gene expression, leading to the development of specific structures and functions.

There are four main types of tissues in the human body:

1. Epithelial tissue: These cells form the lining of organs, glands, and other body surfaces. They provide a barrier against external substances, regulate the exchange of materials between the body and the environment, and produce secretions.
2. Connective tissue: This tissue provides support, structure, and protection to the body. It can be further divided into two subtypes: loose connective tissue (found in areas like skin and fat) and dense connective tissue (found in bones, tendons, and ligaments).
3. Muscle tissue: This tissue is responsible for movement and contraction. There are three types: skeletal muscle (voluntary movement), smooth muscle (involuntary movement), and cardiac muscle (heart muscle).
4. Nervous tissue: This tissue is responsible for transmitting and processing information. It's composed of neurons (nerve cells) and glial cells (support cells).

Key characteristics of each tissue type include:

* Epithelial tissue: tightly packed cells with little extracellular space, often with specialized structures like cilia or microvilli
* Connective tissue: cells are often scattered among a matrix of fibers and ground substance
* Muscle tissue: cells are highly specialized for contraction, with specific structures like sarcomeres and myofibrils
* Nervous tissue: cells are highly specialized for transmission and processing of information, with complex structures like dendrites, axons, and synapses

These tissue types can combine to form organs, which are structures that perform specific functions. For example, the skin is an organ composed of epithelial and connective tissue that provides a barrier against the environment.

Systems, on the other hand, are groups of organs that work together to perform specific functions. For example, the circulatory system includes the heart, blood vessels, and blood, which work together to transport oxygen and nutrients throughout the body.
