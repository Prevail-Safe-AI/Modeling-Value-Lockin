Correlated wave functions are a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics, and understanding them is crucial to grasping entanglement.

In quantum mechanics, wave functions describe the quantum state of a system. A wave function is a mathematical function that encodes the probability of finding a system in a particular state. When two or more particles are entangled, their wave functions become correlated, meaning that the state of one particle is dependent on the state of the other particles.

Correlated wave functions encode interdependent state in entanglement by describing the joint probability distribution of the system. In other words, the wave function of the entire system is a function of the wave functions of the individual particles. This means that the state of one particle is not independent of the state of the other particles, but is instead linked to them through the entanglement.

To illustrate this, consider a simple example. Imagine two particles, A and B, that are entangled in such a way that their spin is correlated. If particle A has a spin of "up", particle B must have a spin of "down", and vice versa. The wave function of the system would describe the joint probability of finding particle A with spin "up" and particle B with spin "down", or finding particle A with spin "down" and particle B with spin "up".

In this case, the correlated wave function encodes the interdependent state of the system, meaning that the state of one particle is dependent on the state of the other particle. This is a fundamental property of entanglement, and it's what allows entangled systems to exhibit non-local behavior.

Some examples of correlated wave functions in action include:

* EPR pairs: Entangled electrons that are separated by large distances, yet remain correlated in their spin.
* Bell states: A set of four entangled states that are used to demonstrate the non-locality of entanglement.
* Quantum teleportation: A process that uses entangled photons to transmit information from one location to another without physical transport of the information.
