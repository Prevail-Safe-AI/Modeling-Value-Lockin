Non-local correlations are a fundamental aspect of quantum mechanics, and understanding them is crucial to grasping quantum entanglement.

In classical physics, correlations between two particles are local, meaning that the state of one particle is determined by its own properties and the properties of the other particle are determined by their own properties, but not by the state of the first particle. In other words, the correlation is mediated by the physical distance between the particles.

In contrast, non-local correlations in quantum mechanics imply that the state of one particle can be instantaneously affected by the state of the other particle, regardless of the distance between them. This effect is known as quantum non-locality or entanglement.

To illustrate this, consider two entangled particles, such as photons, that are separated by a large distance. If something happens to one photon, such as being measured or rotated, the state of the other photon is instantly affected, even if it's on the other side of the universe. This is not because the information is transmitted between the particles, but because the particles are connected in a way that transcends space and time.

Non-local correlations are not just a curiosity; they have been experimentally verified numerous times and are a fundamental aspect of quantum mechanics. They have also been used to develop quantum cryptography and quantum teleportation, which have potential applications in secure communication and information transfer.

In summary, non-local correlations in the context of quantum entanglement refer to the instantaneous connection between two particles that allows their states to be correlated, regardless of distance. This connection is a fundamental aspect of quantum mechanics and has been experimentally verified.
