Systems efficiency metrics aim to quantify the performance, resource usage, and cost of a system to evaluate its overall efficiency. Here's a breakdown of each aspect:

1. **Performance**: This refers to the system's ability to achieve its intended goals or objectives. Performance metrics can include:
	* Throughput (e.g., number of transactions processed per hour)
	* Response time (e.g., average time to complete a task)
	* Accuracy (e.g., error rate)
	* Availability (e.g., uptime percentage)
2. **Resource usage**: This encompasses the consumption of resources required to operate the system, including:
	* Financial resources: costs associated with hardware, software, maintenance, and personnel
	* Human resources: personnel time and effort required to manage and maintain the system
	* Physical resources: energy consumption, storage capacity, and network bandwidth
	* Other resources: such as database storage, memory, and processing power
3. **Cost**: This refers to the financial expenditures required to operate and maintain the system. Cost metrics can include:
	* Total cost of ownership (TCO)
	* Return on investment (ROI)
	* Cost per transaction or unit of output

To assess systems efficiency, various frameworks and metrics are used. Some common ones include:

1. **Return on Investment (ROI)**: calculates the return on investment as a percentage of the initial investment.
2. **Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)**: evaluates the costs and benefits of a system to determine its efficiency.
3. **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)**: calculates the total cost of owning and maintaining a system over its lifespan.
4. **Efficiency Ratio**: calculates the ratio of output to input, such as throughput to resource consumption.
5. **Balanced Scorecard**: a framework that uses four perspectives (financial, customer, internal processes, and learning and growth) to evaluate organizational performance.

When evaluating systems efficiency, the importance of each aspect can vary depending on the specific context and goals.
