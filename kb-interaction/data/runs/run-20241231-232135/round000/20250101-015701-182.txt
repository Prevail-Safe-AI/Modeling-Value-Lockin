A wave function is a mathematical description of a quantum system, which encodes all the information about the system's properties, such as position, momentum, and energy. In essence, it's a probability distribution that describes the likelihood of finding the system in a particular state.

Correlated wave function linking refers to the phenomenon where two or more quantum systems, initially independent, become connected through an interaction. This interaction causes their wave functions to become "entangled," meaning that the state of one system is now dependent on the state of the other, even when they're separated by large distances.

Think of it like a dance: before the interaction, each system is like a solo dancer, moving independently. But when they interact, they become connected, and their movements become synchronized. Just as the dancers' movements are correlated, the wave functions of the quantum systems become correlated, too.

This correlation is what gives rise to quantum entanglement. When you measure the state of one system, it instantly affects the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them. This is because the wave functions are still "linked" through the interaction, allowing them to influence each other.

In summary, correlated wave function linking is the process by which two or more quantum systems become connected through an interaction, resulting in their wave functions becoming entangled and allowing for instantaneous correlations between their states.
