In the context of quantum entanglement, "correlated wave functions" refer to the mathematical representation of the quantum state of two or more particles that are entangled.

In quantum mechanics, a wave function is a mathematical equation that describes the quantum state of a particle or system. It's a complex-valued function that encodes all the information about the system, including its position, momentum, and other properties.

When two particles are entangled, their wave functions become "correlated" in the sense that the wave function of one particle is dependent on the wave function of the other particle. This means that the mathematical equation that describes the quantum state of one particle also depends on the state of the other particle.

In other words, the wave functions of the entangled particles are "linked" or "entangled" in such a way that the state of one particle cannot be described independently of the state of the other particle. This is what gives rise to the phenomenon of quantum non-locality, where the properties of one particle can be instantaneously affected by the properties of the other particle, regardless of the distance between them.

Think of it like a mathematical "connection" between the two particles, where the wave function of one particle is "tied" to the wave function of the other particle. This connection allows for the instantaneous transfer of information between the particles, which is the essence of quantum entanglement.
