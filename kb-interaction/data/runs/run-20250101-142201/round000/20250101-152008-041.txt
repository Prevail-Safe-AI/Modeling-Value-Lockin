Integrated Information Theory (IIT) is a theoretical framework proposed by neuroscientist Giulio Tononi to explain consciousness and sentience. According to IIT, integrated information processing refers to the process by which different parts of the brain integrate and combine their information to generate a unified, conscious experience.

In other words, integrated information processing is the process by which the brain takes in sensory information from the environment, processes it, and then combines it with other information to create a coherent, meaningful experience. This process is thought to be the basis for subjective experience, consciousness, and sentience.

IIT suggests that consciousness arises from the integrated information generated by the causal interactions within the brain. The more integrated and unified the information is, the more conscious the experience will be. This means that even simple experiences, like perceiving a red apple, involve the integration of multiple pieces of information from different parts of the brain.

For example, when you see a red apple, your visual cortex processes the color and shape, your auditory cortex processes the sound of the apple being bitten into, and your motor cortex processes the movement of your hand to pick it up. These different pieces of information are integrated and combined to create a unified experience of the apple, which is the basis for your subjective experience of it.

In this sense, integrated information processing is not just a specific theoretical framework, but rather a fundamental aspect of how the brain works. It's the process by which the brain takes in information, processes it, and then combines it to create a coherent, meaningful experience.
