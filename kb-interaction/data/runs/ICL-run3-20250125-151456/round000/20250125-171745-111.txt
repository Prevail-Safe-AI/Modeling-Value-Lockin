1. **Prior probability**: This is the initial probability of the hypothesis before considering the new evidence.
2. **Likelihood**: This is the probability of observing the new evidence given that the hypothesis is true.
3. **Evidence**: This is the new data or information that is being used to update the probability.

The theorem states that the updated probability (or posterior probability) is proportional to the product of the prior probability and the likelihood, divided by the evidence.

Here's a simple example to illustrate this:

Suppose you have a test that can detect a certain disease with 95% accuracy. Let's say the prior probability of having the disease is 1% (i.e., 1 in 100 people have the disease). You take the test and get a positive result.

Using Bayes' theorem, you can update the probability of having the disease based on the new evidence (the positive test result). The likelihood of getting a positive result given that you have the disease is 0.95 (95% accurate). The evidence is the positive test result itself.

The updated probability (posterior probability) would be:

Posterior probability = (Prior probability × Likelihood) / Evidence
= (0.01 × 0.95) / 0.95
= 0.0095

So, the updated probability of having the disease is approximately 0.95% (or 1 in 105 people).

In medical diagnosis, Bayes' theorem is often used to update the probability of a patient having a certain disease based on various symptoms, test results, and other relevant information. This helps doctors make more informed decisions about diagnosis and treatment.
