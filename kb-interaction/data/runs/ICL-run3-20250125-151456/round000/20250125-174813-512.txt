Let's break down the terms:

1. **Histone modification**: Histones are proteins that DNA wraps around to form chromatin, the complex of DNA and proteins that make up chromosomes. Histone modification refers to the addition of various chemical groups (e.g., methyl, acetyl, or phosphate groups) to the histone proteins. These modifications can either relax or compact chromatin, affecting gene expression. For example, histone 3 lysine 27 trimethylation (H3K27me3) is a repressive mark that can silence gene expression.
2. **DNA methylation**: DNA methylation is the addition of a methyl group to the cytosine residue in a CpG dinucleotide. This modification is typically associated with gene silencing and can be inherited through cell division. DNA methylation can also be removed through a process called demethylation.
3. **ncRNA (non-coding RNA)**: ncRNAs are RNA molecules that don't code for proteins. They can play various roles in gene regulation, including chromatin remodeling, transcriptional silencing, and RNA-mediated gene silencing.

Now, let's discuss how these components interact to lead to epigenetic inheritance:

The epigenetic inheritance process involves the transmission of gene expression patterns from one cell generation to the next without a change in the underlying DNA sequence. Here's a simplified overview:

1. **Chromatin remodeling**: Histone modifications and DNA methylation can alter chromatin structure, making genes more or less accessible to transcription factors. ncRNAs can also interact with chromatin to regulate gene expression.
2. **Epigenetic marks**: Histone modifications and DNA methylation create epigenetic marks that can be inherited through cell division. These marks can be maintained or modified by epigenetic enzymes, such as histone modifiers and DNA methyltransferases.
3. **ncRNA-mediated gene silencing**: ncRNAs can bind to specific DNA sequences and recruit histone-modifying enzymes or DNA methyltransferases to silence gene expression. This process can be inherited through cell division.
4. **Epigenetic inheritance**: When a cell divides, the epigenetic marks, including histone modifications and DNA methylation, are passed on to the daughter cells. This ensures that the gene expression patterns are maintained, even if the underlying DNA sequence remains unchanged.

* Histone modifications can create binding sites for ncRNAs, which can then recruit histone-modifying enzymes to maintain or modify the epigenetic marks.

* ncRNAs can interact with histones and DNA to regulate chromatin structure and gene expression, influencing the epigenetic marks.
