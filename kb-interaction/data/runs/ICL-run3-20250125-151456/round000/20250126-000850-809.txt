In Bayesian inference, a prior distribution is a probability distribution that represents the analyst's initial beliefs or uncertainty about the parameters of a statistical model before observing any data. In other words, it's a way to quantify the analyst's subjective knowledge or bias about the model before seeing the data.

In Bayesian inference, the prior distribution is combined with the likelihood function (which describes the probability of observing the data given the model) to produce a posterior distribution. The posterior distribution represents the updated beliefs or uncertainty about the model parameters after observing the data.

The key idea is that the prior distribution provides a "starting point" for the analysis, and the data then updates those initial beliefs. The prior distribution can be thought of as a "prior guess" or "initial hypothesis" that is then refined or updated based on the data.
