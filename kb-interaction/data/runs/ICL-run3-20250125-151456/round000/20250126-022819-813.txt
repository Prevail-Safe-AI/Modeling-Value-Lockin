"Cognitive biases" refer to systematic errors in thinking, judgment, or decision-making that occur due to mental shortcuts, emotional influences, or other factors that affect our perception, processing, and interpretation of information. These biases can lead to flawed or inaccurate conclusions, and they can be present in both conscious and unconscious thought processes.

Here are some examples of cognitive biases that might influence our thought processes and decision-making:

1. Confirmation bias: The tendency to seek out information that confirms our existing beliefs or opinions, while ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence.
2. Anchoring bias: The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information we receive, even if it's incomplete or inaccurate, when making decisions.
3. Availability heuristic: The tendency to overestimate the importance or likelihood of information that is readily available or easily remembered, while underestimating the importance or likelihood of information that is less accessible.
4. Hindsight bias: The tendency to believe, after an event has occurred, that it was predictable and that we would have predicted it, even if we didn't.
5. Loss aversion: The tendency to prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains, which can lead to risk-averse decision-making.
6. Framing effect: The tendency to be influenced by the way information is presented, rather than the information itself, when making decisions.
7. Sunk cost fallacy: The tendency to continue investing time, money, or effort into something because of the resources already committed, even if it no longer makes sense to do so.
8. Social proof: The tendency to follow the actions of others, even if it's not necessarily the best decision, because we assume that others have more knowledge or expertise.
9. Emotional reasoning: The tendency to believe that our emotions reflect reality, rather than recognizing that emotions can be influenced by biases and other factors.
10. Illusion of control: The tendency to overestimate our ability to control events or outcomes, which can lead to overconfidence and poor decision-making.

These are just a few examples, but there are many more cognitive biases that can influence our thought processes and decision-making.
