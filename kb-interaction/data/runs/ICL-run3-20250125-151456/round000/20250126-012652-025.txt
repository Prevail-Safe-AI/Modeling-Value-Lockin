To understand this better, let's break down Bayes' theorem:

P(A|B) = P(B|A) \* P(A) / P(B)

Where:

* P(A|B) is the posterior probability (the updated probability of A given the new data B)
* P(B|A) is the likelihood (the probability of observing B given A)
* P(A) is the prior probability (the initial probability of A)
* P(B) is the probability of observing B (regardless of A)

The key insight here is that the posterior probability (P(A|B)) is proportional to the likelihood (P(B|A)) times the prior probability (P(A)). This is where the "proportional weights" come in.

In other words, the prior probability is updated in proportion to the likelihood of the new data. The more likely the new data is given the prior probability, the more the prior probability is updated towards the likelihood.

The "proportional" relationship means that the update is directly related to the likelihood. The more extreme the likelihood, the more extreme the update will be. This is why Bayes' theorem is often referred to as a "proportional updating" rule.

For example, if the likelihood is very high, the prior probability will be updated towards the likelihood, making the posterior probability very close to the likelihood. On the other hand, if the likelihood is very low, the prior probability will be updated away from the likelihood, making the posterior probability very close to the prior probability.
