In Bayesian inference, identifiability refers to the property of a statistical model that ensures the parameters of interest can be uniquely estimated from the data. In other words, identifiability means that the model is able to distinguish between different values of the parameters, given the observed data.

There are several reasons why identifiability is crucial for unique parameter estimation in Bayesian models:

1. **Avoiding ambiguity**: Without identifiability, the model may not be able to distinguish between different values of the parameters, leading to ambiguity in the estimation process. This can result in multiple solutions or non-unique estimates, which can be problematic for inference.
2. **Preventing over-estimation**: Identifiability helps prevent over-estimation of the parameters, which can occur when the model is not able to distinguish between different values. Over-estimation can lead to inaccurate inference and poor model performance.
3. **Enabling efficient estimation**: Identifiability enables efficient estimation of the parameters, as the model can focus on the most informative data points and avoid redundant information.
4. **Facilitating model comparison**: Identifiability allows for meaningful comparison between different models, as it ensures that the parameters are being estimated in a consistent and unique manner.

In Bayesian models, identifiability is often achieved through the use of informative priors, which provide additional information about the parameters and help to distinguish between different values. Additionally, the choice of likelihood function and the specification of the model structure can also impact identifiability.
