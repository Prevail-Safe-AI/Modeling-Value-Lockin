**What is "unmeasured confounding"?**

Unmeasured confounding, also known as residual confounding, refers to the situation where there are factors that affect both the outcome of interest and the treatment or exposure being studied, but these factors are not accounted for in the analysis. In other words, there are variables that are associated with both the treatment and the outcome, but are not measured or controlled for in the study.

**How does it happen in RCTs?**

In RCTs, unmeasured confounding can occur due to various reasons:

1. **Selection bias**: Participants may be selected for the study based on certain characteristics that are not measured, but which affect the outcome. For example, a study on the effectiveness of a new medication might only recruit participants who are more likely to respond well to the medication.
2. **Performance bias**: The way the treatment is administered or the way participants are monitored can vary between groups, leading to differences in the outcome that are not due to the treatment itself.
3. **Detection bias**: The way outcomes are measured or diagnosed can differ between groups, leading to biased results.
4. **Attrition bias**: Participants may drop out of the study at different rates between groups, leading to biased results.
5. **Unmeasured variables**: There may be variables that affect both the treatment and the outcome, but are not measured or accounted for in the study.

**How does it affect the results?**

Unmeasured confounding can lead to biased estimates of the treatment effect, which can result in:

1. **Overestimation or underestimation of the treatment effect**: The true effect of the treatment may be different from what is observed due to the confounding variables.
2. **Inaccurate conclusions**: The study may conclude that the treatment is effective or ineffective when, in reality, the results are due to the confounding variables.
3. **Loss of precision**: The study may have lower precision or power to detect the treatment effect due to the confounding variables.

To mitigate unmeasured confounding, researchers can use various strategies, such as:

1. **Measuring additional variables**: Collecting more data on potential confounding variables to account for them in the analysis.
2. **Using instrumental variables**: Identifying an instrumental variable that affects the treatment but not the outcome, allowing for the estimation of the treatment effect.
3. **Using propensity scores**: Creating a score that reflects the probability of receiving the treatment based on measured confounding variables, and then matching participants based on these scores.
4. **Using sensitivity analysis**: Conducting sensitivity analyses to assess the potential impact of unmeasured confounding on the results.
