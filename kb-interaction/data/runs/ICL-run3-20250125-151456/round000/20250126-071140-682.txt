Here are some concrete examples of how cognitive heuristics can lead to subtle biases in research:

1. **Confirmation bias**: Researchers may be more likely to seek out data that confirms their hypothesis, rather than seeking out data that contradicts it. This can lead to a biased sample selection or data analysis.
2. **Anchoring bias**: Researchers may rely too heavily on initial impressions or early data, which can influence their subsequent decisions and analysis. For example, if an initial analysis shows a significant effect, the researcher may be more likely to interpret subsequent data in a way that supports that effect, even if it's not justified.
3. **Hindsight bias**: Researchers may overestimate the likelihood of an event or outcome after it has occurred. This can lead to biased interpretations of data or overconfidence in the results.
4. **Availability heuristic**: Researchers may overestimate the importance or likelihood of an event or outcome based on how easily examples come to mind. For example, if a researcher is familiar with a particular study that found a significant effect, they may overestimate the likelihood of similar effects in other studies.
5. **Framing effect**: Researchers may be influenced by the way information is presented, rather than the information itself. For example, a study may present data in a way that emphasizes the positive effects of a treatment, rather than the negative effects.

These biases can manifest in various ways throughout the research process, including:

* Research design: Cognitive heuristics can influence the selection of research questions, study populations, or data collection methods.
