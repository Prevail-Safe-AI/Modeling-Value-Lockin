Bayes' theorem is a fundamental concept in probability theory, and it's used to update the probability of a hypothesis or event based on new evidence or data. The theorem is named after Thomas Bayes, an 18th-century English mathematician and Presbyterian minister.

The theorem states that the posterior probability (the updated probability) is equal to the prior probability (the initial probability) multiplied by the likelihood (the probability of the new evidence given the hypothesis) and then divided by the marginal likelihood (the probability of the new evidence).

Mathematically, this can be represented as:

Posterior probability = (Prior probability Ã— Likelihood) / Marginal likelihood

The prior probability represents the initial probability of the hypothesis or event before considering the new evidence. The likelihood represents the probability of the new evidence given the hypothesis. The marginal likelihood represents the probability of the new evidence regardless of the hypothesis.

In other words, Bayes' theorem updates the prior probability by multiplying it by the likelihood and then normalizing the result by dividing it by the marginal likelihood. This ensures that the posterior probability is a valid probability distribution, meaning it sums to 1.
