The relationship between coherence, consistency, and non-contradictory conclusions is crucial for logical soundness. Let's break them down:

1. **Coherence**: Coherence refers to the logical connection between the premises and the conclusion. It ensures that the argument is well-structured and that each step logically follows from the previous one. In other words, coherence is about the internal consistency of the argument.
2. **Consistency**: Consistency is about ensuring that the premises and the conclusion do not contradict each other. It means that the argument does not contain any logical contradictions or inconsistencies.
3. **Non-contradictory conclusions**: A non-contradictory conclusion is one that does not contradict any other conclusion or piece of information. This means that the conclusion is consistent with all the available evidence and does not lead to logical contradictions.

These three aspects are interdependent and work together to ensure logical soundness. Here's how:

* Coherence is essential for consistency, as a coherent argument is more likely to be consistent. If the premises and conclusion are logically connected, it's easier to identify potential inconsistencies.
* Consistency is crucial for non-contradictory conclusions. If the premises and conclusion are consistent, it's less likely that the conclusion will contradict other information.
* Non-contradictory conclusions rely on both coherence and consistency. A conclusion that is non-contradictory must be both logically connected to the premises (coherence) and consistent with all available evidence (consistency).

To illustrate this, consider an argument:

Premise 1: All humans are mortal.
Premise 2: Socrates is human.
Conclusion: Socrates is mortal.

This argument is coherent because the conclusion logically follows from the premises. It's consistent because the premises don't contradict each other. And the conclusion is non-contradictory because it doesn't contradict any other information.

On the other hand, an argument that is incoherent, inconsistent, or has a contradictory conclusion would not be logically sound.

Regarding your question about what it means for a conclusion to be "non-contradictory," it indeed means that the conclusion does not contradict any other conclusion or piece of information. This can include:

* Contradicting other conclusions drawn from the same premises
* Contradicting other evidence or information
* Contradicting established facts or theories

In summary, coherence, consistency, and non-contradictory conclusions are all essential for logical soundness. They work together to ensure that an argument is well-structured, free from contradictions, and consistent with all available evidence.
