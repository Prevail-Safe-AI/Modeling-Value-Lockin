Logical soundness refers to the structure and form of an argument, ensuring that it follows a valid logical sequence of reasoning. In other words, a logically sound argument is one where the conclusion follows necessarily and inevitably from the premises, without any logical fallacies or inconsistencies. This means that the argument is free from errors in reasoning, such as circular reasoning, ad hominem attacks, or false dichotomies.

However, logical soundness does not guarantee the truth or accuracy of the argument's claims. An argument can be logically sound but still contain false or inaccurate information. For example:

Argument: "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. Therefore, Socrates is mortal."

This argument is logically sound because it follows a valid logical structure: the conclusion logically follows from the premises. However, the argument's claims may not be true or accurate. * Accuracy refers to the precision and correctness of the argument's claims, whether they are true or not.
