Epigenetics is the study of heritable changes in gene function that occur without a change in the underlying DNA sequence. These changes can affect how genes are expressed, or turned on or off, and can be influenced by a variety of factors, including environmental factors, lifestyle, and genetics.

In the context of gene expression, epigenetics plays a crucial role in regulating the activity of genes. There are several mechanisms by which epigenetics can regulate gene expression, but two of the most important are histone modifications and DNA methylation.

Histone modifications refer to the addition of various chemical groups to the histone proteins that DNA wraps around to form chromatin. These modifications can either relax or compact chromatin, making it more or less accessible to transcription factors and other proteins that regulate gene expression. For example, the addition of acetyl groups to histones can relax chromatin and make it more accessible to transcription factors, while the addition of methyl groups can compact chromatin and make it less accessible.

DNA methylation, on the other hand, is the addition of a methyl group to the cytosine residue in a CpG dinucleotide. This methylation can silence gene expression by recruiting proteins that bind to methylated DNA and prevent transcription factors from accessing the gene. DNA methylation is often found in gene promoter regions and can be used to silence genes that are not needed in a particular cell type or at a particular time.
