In the context of logical coherence, soundness refers to the quality of an argument that ensures its conclusions logically follow from its premises, without any logical fallacies or contradictions. In other words, a sound argument is one that is free from logical errors, and its conclusions are necessarily true if its premises are true.

To break it down further, soundness involves several key aspects:

1. **Validity**: The argument's structure is logically correct, meaning that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises.
2. **Consistency**: The argument's premises do not contradict each other or the conclusion.
3. **Relevance**: The premises are relevant to the conclusion, and the argument is not using unrelated or irrelevant information.
4. **Absence of fallacies**: The argument does not contain any logical fallacies, such as ad hominem attacks, straw man arguments, or false dichotomies.

When an argument is sound, it means that if the premises are true, the conclusion must also be true. This is because the argument's structure is logically correct, and it does not contain any flaws that would lead to an invalid or false conclusion.
