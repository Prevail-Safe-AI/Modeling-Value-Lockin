Instead, it's a highly interdisciplinary field that integrates these four disciplines in a more intertwined and iterative process.

Here's a high-level overview of how bioinformatics combines these disciplines:

1. **Biology**: Bioinformatics starts with a biological question or problem, such as understanding the function of a particular gene or protein, identifying disease-causing mutations, or analyzing the behavior of a biological system. Biologists provide the context and the biological knowledge that informs the analysis.
2. **Computer Science**: Computer scientists contribute algorithms, data structures, and software engineering principles to develop the tools and methods needed to analyze and process large biological datasets. They also design and implement the computational infrastructure that supports bioinformatics research.
3. **Mathematics**: Mathematicians bring mathematical techniques and theories to bioinformatics, such as linear algebra, calculus, probability, and statistics. These mathematical tools are used to model biological systems, analyze data, and develop algorithms for solving biological problems.
4. **Statistics**: Statisticians provide the statistical methods and theories needed to analyze and interpret the large datasets generated in bioinformatics. They help to develop and apply statistical models, test hypotheses, and make predictions about biological systems.

In bioinformatics, these four disciplines are combined in an iterative process that involves:

1. **Data generation**: Biologists generate biological data, such as DNA or protein sequences, gene expression data, or imaging data.
2. **Data analysis**: Computer scientists and statisticians develop algorithms and software to analyze the data, using mathematical and statistical techniques to identify patterns, trends, and correlations.
3. **Modeling and simulation**: Mathematicians and computer scientists use mathematical models and simulations to understand the biological systems and processes that generated the data.
4. **Interpretation and validation**: Biologists and statisticians interpret the results of the analysis and validation, using their biological knowledge to understand the implications of the findings.

Throughout this process, the four disciplines are integrated and iterated upon, with each step informing and refining the others. This integration is what enables bioinformatics to tackle complex biological problems and make new discoveries.
