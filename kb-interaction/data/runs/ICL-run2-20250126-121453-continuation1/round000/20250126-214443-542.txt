Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking, judgment, or decision-making that occur due to mental shortcuts, emotional influences, or other factors that deviate from rational, objective thinking. These biases can be triggered by various factors, such as:

1. Limited information or incomplete knowledge
2. Emotional states, like fear, anxiety, or excitement
3. Social pressures, like conformity or peer influence
4. Cognitive limitations, like attention, memory, or processing capacity

Cognitive biases can affect various aspects of human reasoning, including:

1. Perception: Biases can influence how we perceive and interpret information, leading to distorted or incomplete understanding.
2. Judgment: Biases can affect our ability to make objective judgments, leading to flawed or biased decisions.
3. Decision-making: Biases can influence our choices, leading to suboptimal or irrational decisions.

Examples of cognitive biases include:

1. Confirmation bias: We tend to seek and accept information that confirms our existing beliefs, while ignoring or rejecting contradictory evidence.
2. Anchoring bias: We tend to rely too heavily on the first piece of information we receive, even if it's incomplete or inaccurate.
3. Availability heuristic: We tend to overestimate the importance or likelihood of information that is readily available or easily remembered.
4. Hindsight bias: We tend to believe, after an event has occurred, that we would have predicted it, even if we didn't.
5. Framing effect: We tend to be influenced by the way information is presented, rather than the information itself.
