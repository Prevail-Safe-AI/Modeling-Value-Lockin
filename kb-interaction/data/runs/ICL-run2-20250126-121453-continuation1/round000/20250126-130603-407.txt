When we talk about logical soundness, we're considering the following aspects:

1. **Validity**: The argument's conclusion logically follows from its premises.
2. **Consistency**: The argument's premises and conclusions are logically consistent with each other.
3. **Coherence**: The argument's components (premises, conclusions, and intermediate steps) are logically connected and make sense.
4. **Accuracy**: The argument's premises and conclusions are based on accurate information and facts.

Now, when we say that logical soundness means being "free from logical fallacies and errors," we're referring to the idea that an argument can be flawed or incorrect due to various reasons. These flaws can be categorized into two main types:

1. **Logical fallacies**: Errors in reasoning that occur due to incorrect assumptions, flawed logic, or misleading language. Examples include ad hominem attacks, straw man arguments, and false dichotomies.
2. **Errors**: Mistakes or inaccuracies in the argument's premises, conclusions, or intermediate steps. These can be due to incomplete information, misunderstandings, or incorrect data.

In the context of statement 91, "logical soundness" means that an argument is free from both logical fallacies and errors. This ensures that the argument is well-reasoned, accurate, and reliable.

To illustrate this, consider a simple example:

Statement: "All cats are mammals, and all mammals are warm-blooded, so all cats are warm-blooded."

This argument is logically sound because:

* It's valid: The conclusion logically follows from the premises.
* It's consistent: The premises and conclusion are logically consistent.
* It's coherent: The components are logically connected and make sense.
* It's accurate: The premises are based on accurate information (cats are mammals, and mammals are warm-blooded).

In contrast, an argument that contains logical fallacies or errors would not be logically sound. For instance:

Statement: "All cats are mammals, and all mammals are warm-blooded, so all dogs are warm-blooded."

This argument is flawed because it contains a logical fallacy (non sequitur) and an error (assuming that all mammals are warm-blooded, which is not true for all mammals).
