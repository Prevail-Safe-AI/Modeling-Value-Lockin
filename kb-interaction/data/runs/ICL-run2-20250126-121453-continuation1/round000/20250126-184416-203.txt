Internal validity refers to the extent to which a study's design and methods ensure that the observed relationships or effects between variables are due to the independent variable (IV) and not to other factors. In other words, internal validity addresses the question: "Are the observed effects caused by the IV, or are they due to other factors?"

In the context of research validity, internal validity is essential because it helps to establish a causal link between the IV and the dependent variable (DV). When a study has high internal validity, it means that the researcher has taken steps to minimize the impact of extraneous variables, such as confounding variables, measurement errors, or biases, on the observed relationships.

For example, in a study examining the effect of a new medication on blood pressure, high internal validity would ensure that the observed decrease in blood pressure is due to the medication and not to other factors, such as changes in diet or exercise habits.
