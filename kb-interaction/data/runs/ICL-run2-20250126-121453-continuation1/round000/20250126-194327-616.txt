Coulomb's Law is a fundamental concept in physics that describes the electrostatic force between two charged objects. It's named after Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, who first formulated it in the 18th century.

The simplified Coulomb's Law formula is:

F = k \* (q1 \* q2) / r^2

Where:

* F is the electrostatic force between the two charges
* k is Coulomb's constant (a fundamental constant of nature)
* q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the two charges
* r is the distance between the centers of the two charges

Now, about the assumptions:

* "Point-like" refers to the fact that the charges are assumed to be infinitesimally small, or "point-like", meaning they have no size or extension in space. In reality, charges are not truly point-like, but this simplification allows us to calculate the force between them more easily.
* "Stationary" means that the charges are assumed to be at rest, not moving. This is a simplification because, in reality, charges can be moving, and their motion can affect the force between them. However, for many practical purposes, the assumption of stationary charges is a good enough approximation.

By assuming point-like and stationary charges, the simplified Coulomb's Law formula becomes a useful tool for calculating the electrostatic force between two charges. It's a great example of how simplifying assumptions can help us understand complex phenomena!
