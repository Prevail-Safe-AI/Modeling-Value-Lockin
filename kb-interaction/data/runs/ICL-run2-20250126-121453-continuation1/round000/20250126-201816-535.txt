Factor analysis is a statistical method used to reduce the dimensionality of a large dataset by identifying underlying factors that explain the correlations among the variables. In factor analysis, it is indeed assumed that the relationships among the observed variables are linear.

In particular, factor analysis assumes that the observed variables can be represented as a linear combination of a smaller set of underlying factors. This means that each observed variable is a weighted sum of the factors, where the weights are the factor loadings.

For example, suppose we have a dataset of exam scores for students, and we want to identify the underlying factors that explain the correlations among the scores. Factor analysis might reveal two underlying factors: "math ability" and "reading ability". The observed exam scores would then be represented as a linear combination of these two factors, with each score being a weighted sum of the math and reading abilities.

The assumption of linearity is necessary for several reasons:

1. **Computational simplicity**: Linear relationships are easier to work with computationally, as they can be represented using simple algebraic equations. This makes it possible to perform efficient calculations and extract meaningful insights from the data.
2. **Interpretability**: Linear relationships are more interpretable than non-linear relationships, as they can be easily visualized and understood. This makes it easier to identify the underlying factors and their relationships with the observed variables.
3. **Model assumptions**: Many statistical models, including factor analysis, rely on assumptions of linearity. If the relationships among the variables are non-linear, these models may not be applicable or may produce biased results.

While the assumption of linearity might seem restrictive, it is a reasonable assumption in many cases. In practice, many real-world relationships are approximately linear over a limited range of values. Additionally, factor analysis can be used to identify non-linear relationships by transforming the data or using non-linear factor analysis techniques.
