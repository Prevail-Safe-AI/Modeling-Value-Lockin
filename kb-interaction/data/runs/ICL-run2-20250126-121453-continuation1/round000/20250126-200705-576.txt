Let's break it down step by step.

**Internal Validity**:
Internal validity refers to the extent to which a study's design and methods ensure that the observed effects are due to the independent variable (the thing being manipulated) and not to other factors. In other words, internal validity is concerned with whether the study is free from confounding variables, measurement errors, and other threats to the validity of the findings.

**External Validity**:
External validity, on the other hand, refers to the extent to which the study's findings can be generalized to other populations, settings, and situations. In other words, external validity is concerned with whether the study's results can be applied beyond the specific context in which the study was conducted.

**Construct Validity**:
Construct validity is a type of validity that deals with the relationship between the theoretical construct (the concept or idea being measured) and the operational definition (the specific measurement or assessment used to measure it). In other words, construct validity is concerned with whether the measurement tool accurately reflects the underlying construct.

Now, let's get back to the statement: "Internal validity affects external validity through construct validity."

**The Connection**:
The idea is that internal validity is closely tied to construct validity. When a study has high internal validity, it means that the measurement tool (the operational definition) is accurately reflecting the underlying construct. This, in turn, increases the confidence that the findings can be generalized to other populations, settings, and situations (i.e., external validity).

Here's why:

1. **Measurement errors**: If a study has low internal validity due to measurement errors, it's likely that the construct validity is also compromised. This means that the measurement tool is not accurately reflecting the underlying construct, which reduces the confidence in the study's findings and makes it harder to generalize them to other contexts.
2. **Confounding variables**: When a study has low internal validity due to confounding variables, it's likely that the construct validity is also affected. This means that the measurement tool is not accurately capturing the underlying construct, which reduces the confidence in the study's findings and makes it harder to generalize them to other contexts.

**Implications**:
The implications of this statement are that:

1. **High internal validity is necessary but not sufficient** for high external validity. You need to ensure that the measurement tool accurately reflects the underlying construct (construct validity) in order to generalize the findings to other contexts.
2. **Construct validity is a critical link** between internal and external validity. If the measurement tool is not accurately reflecting the underlying construct, it's unlikely that the study's findings can be generalized to other contexts.

**Assumptions**:
The assumptions behind this statement are:

1. **Theoretical construct**: The study is based on a clear and well-defined theoretical construct.
2. **Measurement tool**: The measurement tool is a valid and reliable operational definition of the theoretical construct.
3. **Internal validity**: The study has high internal validity, meaning that the measurement tool is accurately reflecting the underlying construct and that the findings are not due to confounding variables or measurement errors.
