Coulomb's Law, named after Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, is a fundamental law in physics that describes the electrostatic force between two charged objects. The law states that the force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

The statement you're uncertain about suggests that Coulomb's Law simplifies calculations by ignoring charge size and shape. This is partially true, but there's more to it.

Coulomb's Law assumes that the charges are point-like, meaning they have no size or dimension. This assumption is a simplification, as real charges do have size and shape. However, this simplification is justified because, at the macroscopic scale, the size and shape of charges are negligible compared to the distance between them.
