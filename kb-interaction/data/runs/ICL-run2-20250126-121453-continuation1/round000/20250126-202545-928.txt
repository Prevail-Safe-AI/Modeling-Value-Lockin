Let's dive deeper into Coulomb's Law and its assumptions.

Coulomb's Law is a fundamental concept in electrostatics that describes the force between two charged particles. The law states that the force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

The assumption of point charges in Coulomb's Law is indeed made for simplicity, but it's not the only reason. Point charges are idealized charges that have zero size and volume, which makes them easier to work with mathematically. By assuming point charges, the calculations become much simpler, and the law can be applied to a wide range of situations.

However, the assumption of point charges is not entirely accurate. Real-world charges have a finite size and volume, which can affect the force between them. This is known as the "finite size effect." In reality, charges are not point-like, but rather have a finite size and shape, which can lead to deviations from the predictions of Coulomb's Law.

The implications of these assumptions on the accuracy of the law are significant. While Coulomb's Law is an excellent approximation for many situations, it's not a perfect model. The finite size effect can lead to errors in the calculation of the force between charges, especially at very small distances or for charges with large sizes.

To account for the finite size effect, more advanced models and calculations are needed. For example, the "polarizable" model takes into account the deformation of the charge distribution due to the electric field, which can lead to more accurate predictions.

In summary, the assumption of point charges in Coulomb's Law is made for simplicity, but it's not the only reason.
