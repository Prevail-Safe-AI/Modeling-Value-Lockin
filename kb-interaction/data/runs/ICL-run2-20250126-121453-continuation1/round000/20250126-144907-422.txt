Coulomb's Law, named after Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, describes the electrostatic force between two charged objects. The law states that the force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

The law is typically written as:

F = k \* (q1 \* q2) / r^2

Where F is the force, k is Coulomb's constant, q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the charges, and r is the distance between the charges.

As you mentioned, Coulomb's Law is often applied to the interaction between two point charges. However, in reality, most objects have finite sizes and shapes, which can affect the electrostatic force between them.

When dealing with real-world objects, Coulomb's Law can still be used as an approximation, but it's important to consider the following:

1. **Shape and size**: The shape and size of the objects can affect the electrostatic force. For example, if two objects have a large surface area in contact, the force can be stronger than expected.
2. **Charge distribution**: The distribution of charge on the surface of the objects can also impact the force. For instance, if the charge is concentrated at a specific point, the force can be stronger than if the charge were evenly distributed.
3. **Polarization**: When a charged object is brought near another object, the latter can become polarized, which can affect the electrostatic force.

To account for these factors, scientists and engineers often use numerical methods, such as finite element methods or boundary element methods, to calculate the electrostatic force between objects with complex shapes and charge distributions.

In some cases, Coulomb's Law can be used as a simplification, assuming that the objects are small compared to the distance between them, and that the charge is evenly distributed. However, this approximation may not be accurate for all situations.
