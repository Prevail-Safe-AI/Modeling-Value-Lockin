When a measurement is made on a quantum system, the wave function collapses, meaning that the system "chooses" one particular state from all the possible states it could be in. This is often referred to as wave function collapse or measurement-induced collapse.

To illustrate this, let's consider a simple example. Imagine you have a coin that can be either heads or tails. In quantum mechanics, the coin's state is described by a wave function that represents the probability of finding the coin in either the heads or tails state. In the same way, when a measurement is made on a quantum system, the wave function collapses to one particular state, and the system "chooses" one particular outcome. This is why, in quantum mechanics, measurement outcomes are definite and not probabilistic.
