In quantum mechanics, a wave function is a mathematical description of a quantum system, such as an electron or a photon. The wave function encodes all the information about the system, including its position, momentum, energy, and other properties. The wave function is a complex-valued function that describes the probability of finding the system in different states.

When a measurement is made on a quantum system, the wave function collapses, meaning that the system "chooses" one particular state from all the possible states it could be in. This is often referred to as wave function collapse or measurement-induced collapse.

To illustrate this, let's consider a simple example. Imagine you have a coin that can be either heads or tails. In quantum mechanics, the coin's state is described by a wave function that represents the probability of finding the coin in either the heads or tails state. Before the coin is measured, the wave function is a superposition of both heads and tails, meaning that the coin is in a mixture of both states.

When you measure the coin, the wave function collapses to one of the two states, either heads or tails. This means that the coin has "chosen" one particular state, and the probability of finding it in that state is now 100%. The other state, which was previously possible, is now impossible.

In the same way, when a measurement is made on a quantum system, the wave function collapses to one particular state, and the system "chooses" one particular outcome. This is why, in quantum mechanics, measurement outcomes are definite and not probabilistic.

Wave function collapse is a fundamental aspect of quantum mechanics, and it's what allows us to make sense of the probabilistic nature of quantum systems. It's a bit counterintuitive, but it's a key feature that distinguishes quantum mechanics from classical mechanics.
