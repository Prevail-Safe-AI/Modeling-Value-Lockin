Checks and balances are a crucial concept in political science, but it's understandable that you're uncertain about how they work in practice.

In a system with checks and balances, power is divided among different branches or levels of government to prevent any one branch or level from becoming too powerful. Here's a simplified example:

* In the US federal system, the legislative branch (Congress) has the power to make laws, the executive branch (the President) has the power to enforce laws, and the judicial branch (the Supreme Court) has the power to interpret laws.
* This separation of powers prevents any one branch from dominating the others. For example, the President can't simply make laws without Congressional approval, and Congress can't appoint judges without the President's approval.
* Federalism, on the other hand, divides power between the federal government and state governments. This prevents the federal government from becoming too powerful and allows states to maintain some autonomy.

In practice, checks and balances work by creating incentives for each branch or level of government to limit the power of the others. For instance:

* The President can veto laws passed by Congress, but Congress can override the veto with a supermajority.
* The Supreme Court can declare laws passed by Congress or actions taken by the President as unconstitutional, but Congress can try to pass new laws to circumvent the Court's decisions.

As for corruption and manipulation, checks and balances can be vulnerable to these issues in several ways:

* Corruption can occur when individuals or groups within a branch or level of government use their power for personal gain or to advance their own interests.
* Manipulation can occur when one branch or level of government tries to influence or control the actions of another branch or level, often through subtle or covert means.

Some common ways that checks and balances can be vulnerable to manipulation or corruption include:

* Lobbying: Special interest groups can try to influence lawmakers or government officials to pass laws or take actions that benefit their interests.
* Gerrymandering: Politicians can manipulate electoral district boundaries to favor their own party or interests.
* Judicial activism: Judges can use their discretion to interpret laws in ways that favor their own political beliefs or interests.

It's worth noting that checks and balances are not a guarantee against tyranny, and they can be imperfect or even fail in certain circumstances. However, they are an important mechanism for promoting accountability, transparency, and power distribution within a system.
