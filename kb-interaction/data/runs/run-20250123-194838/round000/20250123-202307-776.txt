Quantum superposition is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that states that a quantum system can exist in multiple states simultaneously. This means that a quantum particle, such as an electron or a photon, can have multiple properties or values at the same time. For example, a quantum particle can be spinning both clockwise and counterclockwise at the same time, or it can be in two different locations simultaneously.

In classical physics, a particle can only be in one definite state at a time. However, in quantum mechanics, the act of measurement itself causes the particle to collapse from a superposition of states to one definite state. This is known as wave function collapse.

Wave function collapse is the process by which the quantum system's wave function, which describes all the possible states the system can be in, collapses to one specific state when measured. Before measurement, the wave function is a probability distribution over all possible states, but after measurement, the wave function collapses to a single state, and the system is said to be in that state.

To illustrate this, imagine a coin that can be either heads or tails. In classical physics, the coin is either heads or tails, but in quantum mechanics, the coin is in a superposition of both heads and tails until it is measured. When it is measured, the wave function collapses, and the coin is either heads or tails, but not both.

The statement "Quantum superposition entails wave function collapse upon measurement" means that when a quantum system is in a superposition of states, the act of measurement causes the system to collapse to one definite state. This is a fundamental aspect of quantum mechanics and has been experimentally verified numerous times.
