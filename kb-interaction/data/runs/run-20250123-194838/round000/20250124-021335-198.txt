Epigenetic regulation is the process by which cells regulate gene expression without altering the underlying DNA sequence. It's a complex process that involves multiple mechanisms, including chromatin remodeling, histone modifications, and DNA methylation.

Chromatin remodeling refers to the process by which chromatin, the complex of DNA and histone proteins, is reorganized to allow or prevent access to specific regions of the genome. This can be achieved through the action of enzymes that slide, eject, or slide-and-eject nucleosomes (the basic units of chromatin) along the DNA molecule.

Histone modifications refer to the addition of various chemical groups to the histone proteins that make up chromatin. These modifications can either relax or compact chromatin, making it more or less accessible to transcription factors and other regulatory proteins. For example, histone acetylation is often associated with relaxed chromatin and increased gene expression, while histone methylation can lead to compacted chromatin and reduced gene expression.

DNA methylation, on the other hand, refers to the addition of a methyl group to the cytosine residue in a CpG dinucleotide. This modification is often associated with gene silencing and can be found in the promoter regions of genes that are not expressed.

Now, when we say that epigenetic regulation "combines" chromatin remodeling, histone modifications, and DNA methylation, we mean that these processes work together to regulate gene expression. For example:

* Chromatin remodeling can create an open chromatin structure that allows transcription factors to access the promoter region of a gene.
* Histone modifications can then be added to the histone proteins to further relax or compact the chromatin, making it more or less accessible to transcription factors.
* DNA methylation can then be added to the promoter region to silence the gene or to the gene body to regulate its expression.

In this way, epigenetic regulation is a highly coordinated process that involves the integration of multiple mechanisms to regulate gene expression. The "combines" in item 72 refers to the idea that these processes work together in a complex interplay to regulate gene expression, rather than being separate and independent processes.
