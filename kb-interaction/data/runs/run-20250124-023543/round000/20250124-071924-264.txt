Item 26 is indeed an interesting topic. The global workspace theory (GWT) was proposed by psychologist Bernard Baars to explain how our brain integrates information from various sensory and cognitive systems to create our conscious experience.

According to GWT, the global workspace is a network of interconnected brain regions, including the prefrontal cortex, parietal lobe, and temporal lobe, that are responsible for processing and integrating information from various sensory and cognitive systems. This network is thought to be responsible for the "binding" of information, which is the process of linking together different features, such as color, shape, and location, to create a unified conscious experience.

For example, when you see a red apple, the global workspace theory suggests that the visual information from your eyes is processed in the visual cortex, while the color information is processed in the color-sensitive areas of the brain. The global workspace then integrates this information, along with other sensory and cognitive information, such as the shape and texture of the apple, to create a unified conscious experience of the apple.

GWT differs from other theories of consciousness, such as integrated information theory (IIT), in its emphasis on the role of attention and the global workspace in integrating information. According to IIT, consciousness arises from the integrated information generated by the causal interactions within the brain, regardless of attention or the global workspace.
