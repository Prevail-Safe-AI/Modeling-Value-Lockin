The theory was first proposed by psychologist Bernard Baars in the 1980s.

According to GWT, the brain has a "workspace" or "global workspace" that is responsible for integrating information from various sensory, motor, and cognitive systems. This workspace is thought to be a network of interconnected regions in the prefrontal cortex, parietal cortex, and temporal cortex that are involved in attention, working memory, and executive functions.

The theory posits that information from various sensory and cognitive systems is initially processed in specialized modules or "bottlenecks" in the brain, such as the visual cortex for processing visual information. However, this information is not consciously accessible until it is globally broadcasted to the workspace, where it can be integrated with other information and made available for conscious awareness.

In other words, the global workspace is thought to be the "hub" where information from different parts of the brain is brought together and integrated to create a unified, conscious experience. GWT is not a specific mechanism or process that has been directly observed, but rather a theoretical framework that attempts to explain the neural correlates of consciousness.
