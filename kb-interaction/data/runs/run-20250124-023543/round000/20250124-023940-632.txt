Kantian ethics, named after the 18th-century philosopher Immanuel Kant, is a moral philosophy that emphasizes the importance of duty and moral principles over personal interests or consequences. According to Kant, an action is morally right if it is motivated by a sense of duty, rather than by the desire to achieve a specific outcome or benefit.

In other words, Kant argues that we should act morally because it is our duty to do so, rather than because it leads to a particular consequence. This means that even if an action has negative consequences, it can still be considered ethical if it is motivated by a sense of duty.

For example, imagine a doctor who is motivated to perform a surgery not because they want to save the patient's life, but because it is their duty as a medical professional to do so. In this case, the doctor's action is considered ethical, even if the patient dies as a result of the surgery, because the doctor was motivated by a sense of duty rather than personal gain or desire.

Kant's idea is that if we act solely out of a sense of duty, we are more likely to make decisions that align with moral principles, rather than being swayed by personal interests or emotions. This approach emphasizes the importance of moral character and the development of virtues, such as honesty, compassion, and fairness.

Regarding the implication that consequences don't play a role in determining the ethical nature of an act, Kant would argue that consequences are important, but they are not the primary consideration. Instead, he would say that we should focus on the moral principles that guide our actions, rather than the outcomes that result from those actions.

In practice, this means that we should consider the moral implications of our actions, rather than just their consequences.
