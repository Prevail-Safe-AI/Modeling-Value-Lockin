To clarify, histones are the proteins that DNA wraps around to form chromatin. Post-translational histone modifications refer to the addition of various chemical groups to the histone proteins after they have been translated from DNA. These modifications can include methylation, acetylation, phosphorylation, and ubiquitination, among others.

These modifications can affect chromatin structure and gene expression in several ways:

1. **Protein recruitment**: Modified histones can serve as binding sites for specific proteins, such as transcription factors, chromatin remodelers, or histone chaperones. These proteins can then interact with the modified histones to regulate chromatin structure and gene expression.
2. **Histone-DNA interactions**: Modified histones can also alter the way they interact with DNA. For example, histone methylation can lead to a more compact chromatin structure, making it more difficult for transcription factors to access the DNA. On the other hand, histone acetylation can lead to a more open chromatin structure, making it easier for transcription factors to access the DNA.
3. **Chromatin remodeling**: Modified histones can also recruit chromatin remodeling complexes, which can alter the chromatin structure by sliding, rotating, or looping the DNA around the histone core.
