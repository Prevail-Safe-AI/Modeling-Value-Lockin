Post-translational histone modifications refer to the chemical changes that occur to histone proteins after they have been translated from DNA into amino acid sequences. Histones are the main protein components of chromatin, which is the complex of DNA and proteins that make up the chromosomes in eukaryotic cells.

In the context of chromatin structure and gene regulation, histones play a crucial role in compacting DNA into a smaller space, making it less accessible to transcription factors and other proteins. This compacted structure is known as chromatin.

Post-translational histone modifications occur when enzymes, such as histone acetyltransferases (HATs) or histone deacetylases (HDACs), add or remove chemical groups (such as acetyl, methyl, or phosphate groups) to the histone proteins. These modifications can alter the structure and function of chromatin, affecting gene expression, DNA repair, and other cellular processes.

For example, histone acetylation can loosen chromatin structure, making it more accessible to transcription factors and allowing for increased gene expression. On the other hand, histone deacetylation can compact chromatin, reducing gene expression.

These post-translational modifications are reversible, meaning that the enzymes can remove the added groups, restoring the original histone structure. This dynamic regulation of histone modifications is crucial for maintaining proper chromatin structure and gene expression, and is often disrupted in diseases such as cancer.
